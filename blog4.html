Digital Divide Widens

School closures caused by the Covid-19 outbreak have impacted over 1.5 billion pupils worldwide. Most schools have turned to e-learning in the face of this unparalleled educational disruption. Not all youngsters, however, have been able to complete their education via the internet. According to UNESCO, half of all pupils who are presently out of school, or nearly 830 million people, do not have access to a computer. Platforms designed for usage on basic mobile phones, such as Funzi and Ubongo, have been one answer for African families. However, in addition to the challenges of using mobile phones for studying, 40% of kids have no access to the Internet at home.

The digital gap is making it more difficult for educators to maintain learning continuity. It also has the potential to worsen existing disparities, as lack of access disproportionately affects the poor.In low-income nations, disparities are particularly pronounced, with 82 percent of students in Sub-Saharan Africa unable to use the internet. Girls are also disproportionately affected. They are the people who are least likely to have access to technology around the world. Children from low-income families are disproportionately affected by the digital divide in high-income countries. Even in the United States, nearly one-fifth of kids in kindergarten through 12th grade do not have access to computers or Internet connections that are fast enough. The bulk of these are from low-income families or ethnic minorities.
